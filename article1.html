Great Vintage Cameras You Can Still Buy

Vintage shooters from every era still worth adding to your collection.

By: Aj Powell - July 22, 2021

Tags: Photography, Camera

Film is about recording moments. Moments that have passed, even as the shutter clicks. It’s no wonder photography is bound so deeply to nostalgia, sending us down memory lane to simpler times. But the hobby — the art — is deeper still; the equipment you use says just as much about your craft as your subjects or the developed, framed end product. For many, that sense of history is best captured and enjoyed through a vintage camera context, and believe us, there’s no shortage of those on the market. So here’s our help: a list of 26 cult vintage shooters that’ll help you find your creative eye, set you apart from the shutterbug crowd and still produce photos that’ll make your (less talented) friends and family envious.

The Shortlist

The Point-and-Shoot: Yashica T4

The T4's 35mm f/3.5 Zeiss Tessar lens is far and away better than the lenses found on many of today’s top-end digital compact cameras. It's great for house parties and day adventures.

Shop Now

The SLR: Nikon F2

For a 35mm camera, it doesn’t get much better than the Nikon F2. It features interchangeable viewfinders, so if it breaks, it’s a simple fix.

Shop Now

The Rangefinder: Fuji GW690III

The GW690III is known as the “Texas Leica” because of its hefty build quality, size and massive 6 x 9 negatives.

Shop Now

Vintage Cameras 101

Where to Buy

eBay: No-brainer. It’s the biggest, it’s the best, but it can also be a bit daunting. Start your search here.

KEH: Buy, sell, trade, repair — when it comes to vintage cameras, KEH does it all.

Buying Tips

  1. Do your research. Read this post, where our camera boffin has done the legwork for you. Read other sites. Read forums. Make sure you find some common prices before taking the plunge.
  2. Skip the pawn shops. And Craigslist, unless you’re a pro.
  3. Run some diagnostics. Typical problems areas that you’ll want to make sure are working: light meter, shutter, film advance, viewfinder, light seals (though imperfect ones might make for interesting shots), controls, lens.
  4. Don't forget the film: You’re going to need some film.
